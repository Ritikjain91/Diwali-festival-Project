Diwali Celebration

Diwali, also known as the Festival of Lights, is one of the most widely celebrated festivals in India. It signifies the triumph of light over darkness and good over evil. Diwali Explore Diwali, also known as Deepavali, is the annual Festival of Lights1. It is held on the darkest night of the Hindu lunar calendar and ushers in new beginnings just before the arrival of the new moon1. The festival gets its name from the row (avali) of clay lamps (deepa) that Indians light outside their homes to symbolize the inner light that protects from spiritual darkness2. This festival is as important to Hindus as the Christmas holiday is to Christians2. Diwali is celebrated by Hindus, Jains, Sikhs, and Newar Buddhists, although for each faith it marks different historical events and stories3. Nonetheless, the festival represents the same symbolic victory of light over darkness, knowledge over ignorance, and good over evil3. Over five days, people take part in festive gatherings, fireworks displays, feasts, and prayer4. Celebrants light rows of traditional clay oil lamps outside their homes to symbolize the victory of light over darkness and knowledge over ignorance4. The specific legends and rituals may vary, but the essence of Diwali remains a celebration of hope, renewal, and the pursuit of spiritual and moral well-being across diverse religious traditions3. It is widely celebrated in India and is observed by Hindus, Sikhs, Jains, and Newar Buddhists around the world1. It is the most important festival of the year in India and for Hindus in particular4. Diwali is celebrated with great enthusiasm and fervor. People clean and decorate their homes, light traditional oil lamps called diyas, and create beautiful rangoli patterns at their doorsteps.

Families come together to share festive meals, exchange gifts, and enjoy the company of loved ones. The air is filled with the sound of firecrackers, symbolizing the victory of light over darkness.
